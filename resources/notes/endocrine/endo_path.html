Disorders of the Endocrine Pancreas

Diabetes mellitus

Hyperglycemia is a key feature of DM, which results from reduced insulin section (type 1) or tissue resistance to insulin (type 2) (see table below). Complications of DM can be divided into neuropathy, microvascular disease (retinopathy, nephropathy), and macrovascular disease (atherosclerosis).

Presentation

  • Hyperglycemia (duh)
  • Polyuria, polydipsia (glucose-induced osmotic diuresis → dehydration), polyphagia, weight loss
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (type 1)
  • Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (type 2)

Pathophysiology

  • Reduced insulin production (type 1)
  • Tissue resistance to insulin (type 2)
  • Unopposed secretion of GH and epinephrine (rarely)
  • Taking glucocorticoids (steroid diabetes)

Chronic complications

  • Nonenzymatic glycation
    • Microvascular complications: diffuse thickening of basement membrane, leading to ...
      • Diabetic nephropathy: Hyalinization in regions surrounded by glomerular capillary loops (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules) → proteinuria (initially microalbuminuria) due to hyalinization of the efferent arteriole causing glomerular hyperfiltration injury & HTN → nephrotic syndrome → chronic kidney disease
        • ACEi and ARBs are renoprotective
      • Diabetic retinopathy: hemorrhage, exudates, microaneurysms, vessel proliferation
      • Glaucoma
      • Diabetic neuropathy: Peripheral neuropathy (loss of pain and vibratory sensation in the legs—characteristic "glove" and "stocking" distribution), autonomic neuropathy (sexual impotence, delayed gastric emptying)
    • Macrovascular complications:
      • Atherosclerosis: Nonenzymatic glycation produces advanced glycosylation end products, which causes changes in collagen deposition in arterial walls and trap LDL, leading to increased lipid deposition.
        • Coronary artery disease
        • Peripheral vascular disease
        • Stroke – commonly due to carotid artery stenosis or arteriosclerosis of the lenticulostriate arteries
  • Osmotic damage: Glucose is converted to sorbitol in organs via aldose reductase, leading to accumulation/damage, with ↓ or absent sorbitol dehydrogenase
    • Diabetic peripheral neuropathy (motor, sensory, and autonomic degeneration; see more details above)
    • Cataracts

Diagnosis

Nonenzymatic glycation of hemoglobin produces HbA1c, which acts as a marker of glucose intake over the preceding 120 days (a RBC lives for 120d). HbA1c > 6.5% fulfills the criteria for the diagnosis of diabetes. HbA1c is also used to monitor glucose control over time, with a goal of HbA1c < 7.0%.

Test Diabetes Criteria
HbA1c ≥ 6.5%
Fasting plasma glucose ≥ 126 mg/dL
Oral glucose tolerance test ≥ 200 mg/dL
Random plasma glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL + sx of hyperglycemia

Treatment

All DM-1 patients need insulin. Insulin is usually given by subcutaneous injection; it can also be given IV in emergency situation (DKA).

For DM-2 patients, diet and exercise should be the first-line therapy. Most require pharmacological treatment, including oral hypoglycemic drugs (metformin, sulfonylurea) and some also need insulin.

Other complications

Mild complications can included fungal infections, particularly Candida albicans, and nonhealing ulcers and wounds.

Severe complications include diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) for DM-1 pts and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state for DM-2 pts.

Diabetic ketoacidosis

Insulin present, ketones presents

Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a complication of DM-1 arising from ↓ insulin supply (from forgetting to take insulin) or ↑ insulin demand (e.g., infections, illnesses, excess alcohol ingestion) precipitating in ketoacidosis. Lack of insulin causes lipolysis and release free fatty acid from adipose tissues.

  • ↓ insulin → ↑ lipolysis → ↑ glycerol and free fatty acids → β‑oxidation of fatty acids → ↑ ketones bodies (β‑hyroxybutyrate > acetoacetate) → anion gap metabolic acidosis
Sx Delirium/psychosis, Kussmaul respirations (rapid, deep breathing)., Abdominal pain/nausea/vomiting, Dehydration. Fruity breath oder (due to exhaled ketones).
Labs ↑ glucose
↑ H+, ↓ HCO3- (anion gap metabolic acidosis)
↑ urine and blood ketone levels
↑ WBC
Normal/↑ serum K+ but depleted intracellular K+ d/t transcellular shift from ↓ insulin and acidosis.
Osmotic diuresis → ↑ K+ loss in urine → total body K+ depletion

Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state

Insulin present, ketones absent

Profound hyperglycemia → excessive osmotic diuresis → dehydration and ↑ serum osmolality → HHS. Classically seen in elderly DM 2 with limited ability to drink.

Sx Thirst, polyuria, lethargy, focal neurologic deficit, seizures.
Labs ↑ glucose (often > 600 mg/dL)
↑ serum osmolality (> 320 mOsm/kg)
Normal pH, no ketones
Normal/↑ serum K+, ↓ intracellular K+
Complications Can progress to coma and death if untreated
Treatment IV fluids, IV insulin, K+
