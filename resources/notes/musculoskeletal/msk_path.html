Fractures of the Pelvis

Injury/Trauma to the Upper Limb

Scaphoid fracture

Pain in the anatomic snuffbox following fall of outstretched hands. High risk of nonunion or avascular necrosis (AVN), which can lead to early-onset arthritis in the wrist. Risk of AVN increase with more proximal the location of fracture d/t retrograde blood supply of hte scaphoid. For acute onset of pain after trauma, it's important to rule out fractures. Surgical tx is reserved for unstable or displaced fractures and involves open reduction and internal fixation with screw.

Metacarpal neck fracture (boxer's fracture) is more commonly seen in the 4th and 5th metacarpals, and they usually occur d/t a direct blow with a closed fist. Most boxer's fractures can be treated nonoperatively with immobilization.

Lateral epicondylitis

Pain at level of elbow either medially or laterally can indicate epicondylitis. Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) is d/t overuse of common extensor tendon by repetitive wrist extension, which often occurs in racket ball players or laborers. The repetitive motions (i.e., backhand shots) may lead to vascular hyperplasia, fibroblast hypertrophy, and disorganized collagen resulting in tendinopathy or tendonitis. This is similar to findings in patellar or Achilles tendonitis. The lateral epicondyle is the origin for several muscles that extend the wrist, which comprise the common extensor tendon. The extensor carpi radialis brevis (ECRB) is stressed during wrist extension and forearm pronation, like when hitting a backhand shot. the ECRB inserts at the base at the metacarpal distally. As a result, resisted extension of the third digit may reproduce the pain.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis.

DDx:

  • Medial epicondylitis (golfer's elbow) - repetitive flexion as seen in forearm shots. Pain is characteristically near the medial epicondyle and is elicited with forearm pronation or wrist flexion.
  • Olecranon bursitis - pain associated with swelling over the olecranon
  • Cervical radiculopathy - associated neck pain
  • Radial head subluxation (Nursemaid's elbow) - sudden pulling or grabbing a child's arm in order to prevent an injury or incident. The annular ligament slips into the radiohumeral joint and becomes trapped between the two joint surfaces, where it remains stuck. Exam will reveal a pronated arm and partially flexed elbow that is held close to the body, as well as tenderness of the radial head.
Treatment

Conservative treatment with rest, NSAIDs, avoidance of aggravating activity.

Rotator Cuff Tear

Presentation
  • Pain in shoulder after trauma, fails to improve with NSAIDs
  • Difficulty reaching overhead
  • Tenderness over lateral aspect of shoulder
  • Pain with abduction and limited ROM

Depending on which tendon of the four rotator cuff muscles was injured, there are additional signs:

  • Weakness with external rotation with the elbow at patient's side (infraspinatus)
  • Pain with holding hand behind back (subscapularis)
  • Weakness and pain with resisted elevation in the plane of hte scapula (supraspinatus)
Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis. MRI can confirm rotator cuff tear.

Treatment

Treatment includes NSAIDs, physical therapy, or surgical repair.

Nononcologic MSK Diseases

Arthropathies

Osteoarthritis (OA)

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Presentation

Common risk factors include age (35-50), sex (female), HLA-DR4, and smoking.

Extra-articular manifestations can be include Caplan syndrome (pulmonary fibrosis or pnneumoconiosis), Felty syndrome (splenomegaly and neutropenia), pleuritis, pericarditis, interstitial lung disease, amyloidosis, Sjögren's syndrome, and scleritis.

Diagnoses

RA is primarily a clinical diagnosis, although labs can be helpful.

Labs:

  • ↑ erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C‑reactive protein (CRP)
  • ⊕︀ rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-CCP protein antibody (ACPA)

Radiography show periarticular osteopenia, marginal erosions, and joint space narrowing.

Treatment
  • Treatments generally consists of disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) such as methotrexate (MTX), sulfasalazine, and hydroxychloroquine.
  • For minor cases, NSAIDs can be added.
  • For severe cases, the addition of TNFα inhibitors (rituximab, lefluonomide) and other biological agents can be used.
  • Steroids can be used for short-term relief.
Prognosis

Psoriatic arthritis

Psoriatic arthritis is an inflammatory seronegative spondyloarthropathy, which involves inflammation of axial skeleton, asymmetric peripheral arthritis, enthesopathy, eye inflammation (uveitis), and a strong association with teh genetic expression of HLA-B27. The other 3 types of spondyloarthritis are ankylosing spondylitis, inflammatory bowel disease, and reactive arthritis.

Presentation

Patients may present with tenderness and swelling of involved joints, tendons, and ligaments. Psoriasis precedes arthritis in most cases. Characteristic exam findings include psoriasis (red patches with silvery scale on extensor surfaces such as elbows), nail pits, scaly sausage digits (dactylitis) caused by tenosynovitis and arthritis, multiple asymmetric joint involvement (including wrists, MCP, PIP, DIP), and arthritis of the spine.

Diagnosis

Primarily a clinical diagnosis.

Lab tests - ⊖︀ RF and ACPA; strong association with HLA-B27 but independently does not support/exclude the diagnosis

Radiographs may show "pencil-in-cup" deformity of the DIP due to boney destruction around joints.

Differential diagnoses:

  • OA - does not present with skin changes and nail involvement
  • RA - no DIP and spine
  • Gout and pseudogout - typically monoarticular, no joint destruction
  • Reactive arthritis - additive, asymmetric arthritis often involving knees, ankles, or feet; may involve fingers and wrists with dactylitis (distinctive feature similar to psoriatic arthritis); typically occures 1-4 wk after infection (e.g., Shigella, Yersinia, and Chlamydia), also present with urethritis, conjunctivitis, or uveitis, nonpainful mucocutaneous ulcers, or keratoderma blennorrhagica.

Neuropathies & Radiculopathies

Neck & Shoulder

Cervical Radiculopathy

Cervical radiculopathy is an important cause of neck pain that can radiate to the upper extremities. Other symptoms may include numbness, tingling, and weakness in the affected arms. Cervical radiculopathy can results from degenerative cervical spondylosis resulting from age-related loss of disc height and formation of spurs at hte uncovertebral joints and facets, decreasing the space avaialable for the exiting nerve root in the neruoforamen.

Disc herniation may be another cause of cervical radiculopathy. Disc herniations are most commonly posterolateral between the posterior edge of hte uncinate and the lateral edge of hte posterio longitudinal ligament (PLL). This similarly causes impingement on the nerve root. The level of disc herniation may be elucidate based on clinical findings.

Diagnosis

MRI can help localize the nerve root compression spinal level. EMG/NCV is performed ot help distinguish peripheral (carpal tunnel) form central (cervical radiculopathy) processes.

DDx can include:

  • thoracic outlet syndrome, and
  • cervical ribs.
Treatment

NSAIDs and physical therapy. Surgery if there's no improvement or if any upper motor neuron sx are present.

Brachial Plexus Lesions

Erb palsy
Presentation

Muscle deficit: Deltoid, supraspinatus, infraspinatus, biceps brachii (Herb gets DIBs on tips)

Functional deficit: Abduction (arm hangs by side), lateral rotation (arm medially rotated), flexion, supination

Pathophysiology

Caused by traction (in infants during delivery) or tear (adult) of upper trunk of brachial plexus (C5-C6).

Klumpke palsy
Presentation

Muscle deficit: Intrinsic hand muscles: lumbricals, interossei, thenar, hypothenar

Functional deficit: Total claw hand: lumbricals normla flex MCP jts and extend DIP and PIP jts

Pathophysiology

Caused by traction or tear of lower trunk of brachial plexus (C8-T1). For infants, upward force on arm during delivery. For adults, trauma (such as grabbing on tree branch after falling).

Diagnoses

DDx:

  • Pancoast tumors: type of lung cancer (usually nonsmall cell) that occur at the pulmonary apex. Pts may present with shoulder pain, weakness, or paresthesias of the hand and upper arm swelling.
  • Erb's palsy
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome
Treatment

Physical therapy.

Thoracic outlet syndrome
Presentation

Muscle deficit: Intrinsic hand muscles: lumbricals, interossei, thenar, hypothenar

Functional deficit: Atrophy of intrinsic hand muscles; ischemia, pain, and edema d/t to vascular compression

Pathophysiology

Caused by compression of lower trunk and subclavian vessels, most commonly within the scalene triangle, cervical rib, pancoast tumor

Winged scapula
Presentation

Muscle deficit: Serratus anterior

Functional deficit: Inability to anchor scapula to thoracic cage → cannot abduct arm above horizontal position

Pathophysiology

Caused by lesion of long thoracic n., C5-C7 roots ("wings of heaven"), axiallary node dissection after mastectomy, stab wounds.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis.

DDx:

  • Long thoracic nerve (C5-C7) injury: typically result from thoracic surgeries or procedures but may also result form a stab wound. Injury to the nerve leads to serratus anterior deficit and the pathognomonic "winged scapula" on exam.
  • Thoracodorsal nerve injury: present similarly but with the additional findings of shoulder abduction and internal rotation deficit.
Treatment

Physical therapy.

Upper Extremities

Carpal tunnel syndrome

When the median nerve is compressed or entrapped under the carpal tunnel, patients may present with pain over the proximal forearm and thenar area with paresthesias in the median nerve distribution. Sx can be worse with repetitive movements and flexion and is also worse at night. Potential comorbidities include DM, hypothyroidism, amyloidosis, hemochromatosis, RA, acromegaly, and pregnancy.

Diagnosis

Often diagnosed clinically, by history and physical exam alone (⊕︀ Phalen and/or Tinel tests). EMG and nerve conduction velocity are largely confirmatory tests.

Treatment

Treatments include wrist splint and NSAIDs. Sx is reserved for refractory cases

Failure to treat carpal tunnel syndrome may lead to irreversible sensory loss in the median nerve distribution, as well as significant wasting of the thenar muscles which, in turn, can cause weak grasps and thumb opposition.

Ulnar nerve entrapment can occur at the elbow (cubital tunnel syndrome) or the wrist (Guyon's canal syndrome); sx occur in the 4th and 5th digits and may be aggravated by pressure from handlebars in cyclists.

De Quervain's tenosynovitis often present in golfers and racket-sport athletes. It may also present postpartum or post-traumatically. The first dorsal extensor compartment contains the abductor pollicis longus and the extensor pollicis brevis. Pain is elicited d/t swelling and thickening of the first dorsal compartment, which may present as a painful bump over the wrist.

Wrist drop

Wrist drop can occur 2° to compression of the radial nerve at the axilla 2° to prolonged use of crutches or falling asleep with the arm draped over a chair (Saturday night palsy).

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis.

C7 radiculopathy will typically have pain in the neck with radiation into middle finger. There is also usually weakness of the triceps muscle, wrist flexion, and extension fingers. Sensory loss involves the middle finger, and loss of the triceps reflex is also seen.

Treatment

Conservative treatment as spontaneous recovery is likely. This can include NSAIDs for pain and PT to maintain range of motion.
