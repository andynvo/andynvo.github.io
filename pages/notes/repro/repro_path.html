Amenorrhea

Primary amenorrhea is definied as either (1) no period by age 14 in the absence of growth or development of 2º sexual characteristics, or (2) no period by the age of 16. Secondary amenorrhea is defined the absence of a period for a length of time equalivent at least 3 cycles or 6 months in a previously menstruating woman.

Amenorrhea can be caused by disorders of:

  • Chromosomal abnormalities: Turner syndrome (most common cause of 1º amenorrhea)
  • Endocrine dysfunction
    • Hypothalamus: low BMI, rough and dry skin, fine hairs on back and buttocks, ↓ FSH, ↓ estradiol. ↓ GnRH pulsatility, intracranial mass interrupting pituitary stalk, acquired hypopituitarisum (Sheehan's syndrome), Kallmann's syndrome → Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism
    • Anterior pituitary: ↑ GnRH, ↓ FSH/LH
    • Ovary: ↑ everything but estrogen and progesterone
    • Androgen insentivity: 46, XY with external female genitalia, scant pubic and axillary hair, and rudimentary vagina; uterus and fallopian tubes absent due to anti-Müllerian hormone from testes. Nl functioning testes within labia majora, need to remove to prevent malignancy. Labs show ↑ testosterone, estrogen, LH.
    • Hypothyroidism: common and can cause amenorrhea, could be consequence of non-functional anterior pituitary
    • Hyperprolactinemia: common and can affect menstural cycle
  • Genital outflow tract obstructution due to:
    • Müllerian agenesis (Mayer-Rokintasky-Küster-Hauser syndrome): lack of uterine development with fully developed 2° sexual characteristics (functional ovaries)
    • Imperforate hymen/Transverse vaginal septum: nl FSH, LH, estradiol. Mostly likely nl uterus, tubes, and ovaries, though can overlap with other Müllerian anomalies. Only sign is on pelvic exam. Eugonadotropic 1º amenorrhea
    • Asherman's syndrome (intrauterine adhesions or intrauterine synechiae): occurs when scar tissue forms inside the uterus and/or the cervix.

Female Infertility

Clinicially, infertility is defined as faliure of a couple to conceive within a period fo time (12 mos if woman < 35 y/o, 6 mos inf woman ≥ 35 y/o. While "infertility" is often used, subfertility is a more accurate diagnosis prior to establishing the cause of the infertility. Another term to be familiar with is fecundability, the probability of a woman to conceive within one month🔴🟡. The "normal" value of fecundability is 20% (according to various sources).

The WHO classify causes of female infertility into 3 classes:

  • Hypogonadtropic hypogonadism (5‑10%)
  • Normogonadotropic normoestrogenic anovulation (70‑85%)
  • Hypergonadotropic hypoestrogenic anovulation (10‑30%)

Treatments for infertility include: oral medication (ex. clomiphene citrate, letrozole), injectable medication (SQ recombinant FSH/LH/hCG), intrauterine insemination, and IVF. When using clomiphene, it is important to slowly step up the dose from 37.5-75 IU/d until serum FSH reaches the threshold concentration. Once this concentration is reached, the dosage is then stepped down back then stopped completely. Clomiphene is administered approximately 4 days after menses starts.

The risks of these include multiple pregnancies, ovarian cysts, and cancer (?).

*PCO is defined ≥12 follicles in each ovary 2‑9 mm in diameter.

Other signs observed in patients with PCSO includes obesity (≥50%), HTN, dyslipidemia, imparied glucose tolerance, DM2, and metabolic syndrome. PCOS is a diagnosis of (reasonable) exclusion. Its differential include:

  • Hypothyroidism
  • Ovarian failure
  • Hyperprolactinemia
  • Hypothalmic
  • Pregnancy
  • Late onset congenital adrenal hyperplasia
  • Androgen-secreting tumor (ovarian, adrenal)
  • Cushing's syndrome
  • Acromegaly

The ones on the left typically present with oligo-/anovulation without hyperandrogenism; the ones on the right typically present oligo-/anovulation with hyperandrogenism.
