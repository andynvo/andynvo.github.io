Reproductive Physiology & Endocrinology

Female Reproductive Pathology

Amenorrhea

Primary amenorrhea is definied as either (1) no period by age 14 in the absence of growth or development of 2º sexual characteristics, or (2) no period by the age of 16. Secondary amenorrhea is defined the absence of a period for a length of time equalivent at least 3 cycles or 6 months in a previously menstruating woman. It should also be recalled that pregnant women also don't menstruate, and so pregnancy should also be on the differential.

Amenorrhea can be caused by disorders of:

  • Chromosomal abnormalities (e.g. Turner syndrome)
  • Endocrine dysfunction
    • Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (↓ GnRH pulsatility, ↓ FSH, ↓ estradiol)
      • Stress, weight, exercise-induced loss of GnRH pulsatility
      • intracranial mass with interruption of the pituitary stalk
      • Acquired hypopituitarism (Sheehan's syndrome)
      • Kallmann's syndrome
    • Hypergondotropic hypogonadism (↑ GnRH pulsatility, ↑ FSH, ↓ estradiol)
      • Gonadal failure
        • Turner syndrome
        • Fragile X
        • Swyer syndrome = XY gonadal dysgenesis
      • Enzyme deficiences
      • Galactosemia
    • Androgen insentivity syndrome
    • Hypothyroidism
    • Hyperprolactinemia (from pituitary adenoma)
  • Genital outflow tract obstructution due to:
    • Müllerian agenesis
    • Imperforate hymen/Transverse vaginal septum
    • Asherman's syndrome

Female Infertility

Clinicially, infertility is defined as faliure of a couple to conceive within a period fo time (12 mos if woman < 35 y/o, 6 mos inf woman ≥ 35 y/o. While "infertility" is often used, subfertility is a more accurate diagnosis prior to establishing the cause of the infertility. Another term to be familiar with is fecundability, the probability of a woman to conceive within one month🔴🟡. The "normal" value of fecundability is 20% (according to various sources).

The WHO classify causes of female infertility into 3 classes:

  • Hypogonadtropic hypogonadism (5‑10%)
  • Normogonadotropic normoestrogenic anovulation (70‑85%)
  • Hypergonadotropic hypoestrogenic anovulation (10‑30%)

Treatment for infertility include: oral medication, injectable medication, intrauterine insemination, and IVF. The risks of these include multiple pregnancies, ovarian cysts, and cancer (?).
