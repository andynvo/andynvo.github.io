Casts in urine

The presence of casts in urine can indicate that hematuria/pyuria is of glomerular or renal tubular origin.
  • RBC casts: glomerulonephritis, HTN emergency
  • WBC casts: tubulointestinal inflammation, actue pyelonephritis, transplant rejection
  • Granular casts: acute tubular necrosis (ATN). Can be "muddy brown" in appearance.
  • Fatty casts: nephrotic syndrome. Associated with "Maltese cross" sign.
  • Waxy casts: end-stage renal disease, CKD
  • Hyaline casts: nonspecific, can be normal finding.
*Note: Hematuria w/o casts → bladder cancer, kidney stones
Pyuria w/o casts → acute cystitis

Sodium Imbalances

Hypervolemic Hyponatremia

Reduced effective circulating volume: unusual cause of hyponatremia (usually hypernatremia since volume is reduced, artificially inflates [Na+]); clinical clues: presence of peripheral edema, low BP, tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension; low urine sodium
Common causes of reduced effective circulating volume include: heart failure, cirrhosis

Euvolemic Hyponatremia

Pseudohyponatremia: when proteins and lipids increases intravascular volume (d/t to osmotic pressure) leading to apparent decrease in [Na+]

Dilutional hyponatremia: associated with diabetes and insuline deficiency; elevated serum glucose leads to water leaving cells

  • serum osmolality is unusually high in these cases
  • for every + 100 mg/dL of glucose, [Na+] drops by 1.6 mmol/L

Syndrome of inappropriate ADH (SIADH): diagnosis of exclusion; associated with pain and nausea; urine osmolaity > 100 mOsm/kg; low serum OsM

Renal Tubular Acidosis

Disorders of the renal tubules that cause normal anion gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis.

There are 3 types of RTA: distal RTA (type 1), proxmial RTA (type 2), and hyperkalemic RTA (type 4). Note there is no type 3. One differentiating feature of these RTA is the urine pH. In distal RTA (type 1), the urine pH > 5.5. In hyperkalemic RTA (type 4), the urine pH < 5.5. Type 2 RTA is variable depending on the [bicarbonate]. Another differentiating feature is the [K+]. In Type 1 and 2 RTA, [K+] is decreased, and in Type 4 (as the name suggests), [K+] is increased.

Glomerular Diseases

Glomerular diseases can be present as either nephrotic syndrome or nephritic syndrome (although in reality, most glomerular diseases are a combination of both to varying degrees). The key feature of nephrotic syndrome is the proteinuria, caused by podocyte damages. Clinically, this is definied by as > 3.5 g/day of protein excreted in urine. The urine is frothy and usually contains fatty casts. In contrast, nephritic syndrome is clincally defined by the hematuria and the presence of RBC casts in urine. This is caued by GBM damage that leads to RBC being filtered into the nephron.
