Growth Adaptations

In general, an organ is in homeostasis with the normal physiologic stress placed upon it. When there is a change in this stress (whether ↑ or ↓), growth adaptation occurs.

  1. Hypertrophy refers to the increase in cell size, by gene activation, protein synthesis, and production of organelles.
  2. Hyperplasia is the increase in cell number, by production of new cells from stem cells.
  3. Atrophy refers to the decrease in cell size (via ubiquitin-proteosome degradation of cytoskeleton and autophagy of cellular components) and cell number (by apoptosis).
  4. Metaplasia is the change in cell type. For example, a change from one type of surface epithelium (squamous, columnar, or urothelial) to another.
  5. Dysplasia is disordered cell growth and most often refers to precancerous growth.
  6. Aplasia is failure of cell production during embryogenesis.
  7. Hypoplasia is decrease in cell production during embryogenesis.

Principles of Neoplasia

Neoplasia

Neoplasia is new tissue growth that is unregulated, irreversible, and monoclonal; these features distinguish it from hyperplasia and repair.

  • Clonality was historically determined by glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) enzyme isoforms, the gene for which is located on the X chromosome.
  • Clonality can also be determined by androgen reeptor isoforms, which are also present on the X chromosome.

Epidemiology

Cancer is the 2nd leading cause of death in both adults and children.

  • The leading cause of death in adults are (1) cardiovascular disease, (2) cancer, and (3) chronic respiratory disease.
  • The leading cause of death in children are (1) accidents, (2) cancer, and (3) congenital defects.

The most common cancers in adults are (1) breast [F]/prostate [M] (2) lung, and (3) colorectal. The most common cause of cancer mortality in adults are (1) lung, (2) breast/prostate, and (3) colorectal.

Carcinogenesis

Cancer formation occurs when there is damage to DNA of stem cells. The damage overcomes DNA repair mechanism, but is not lethal.Carcinogens are agents that damage DNA, increasing the risk for cancer. Important carcinogens include chemicals, oncogenic viruses, and radiation.

DNA mutations eventually disrupt key regulatory systems, allowing for tumor promotion (growth) and progression (spread). Disrupted system include proto-oncogenesis, tumor suppressor genes, and regulators of apoptosis.

Oncogenesis

Proto-oncogenesis are essential for cell growth and differentiation,; mutations of proto-oncogenes form oncogenes that lead to unregulated cellular growth.

Categories of oncogenes include growth factors, growth factor receptors, signal transducers, nuclear regulators, and cell cycle regulators (Table 3.3).

Tumor Suppressor Genes

Regulate cell growth and, hence, decrease the risk of tumor formation; p53 and Rb (retinoblastoma) are classic examples. p53 and Rb regulate progression from G1 to S phase.

  • p53 slows down cell cycle progression in response to DNA damage and upregulates DNA repair enzymes. If DNA repair is not possible, p53 induces apoptosis (p53 ↑ BAX ⊣ Bcl2 ⇒ cytochrome c release from mitochondria)
    • Both copies of p53 gene must be knocked out for a tumor formation (two-hit hypothesis)
    • Seen in > 50% of cancers.
    • Germline mutations results in Li-Fraumeni syndrome, characterized by propensity to develop multiple types of carcinomas and sarcomas.
  • Rb "holds" the E2F transcription factor, which is necessary for transition to the S phase. E2F is released when RB is phosphorylated by the cyclinD/cyclin-dependent kinase 4 (CDK4) complex. Rb mutation results in constitutively free E2F, allowing progression through the cell cycle and uncontrolled growth of cells.
    • Sporadic mutation is characterized by unilateral retinoblastoma.
    • Germline mutation results in familial retinoblastoma, characterized by bilateral retinoblastoma and osteosarcoma.

Regulators of Apoptosis

Definition: proteins that prevent apoptosis in normal cells, but promote apoptosis in mutated cells whose DNA cannot be repairs.

Example: Bcl2 normally stabilizes the mitochondrial membrane, blocking release of cytochrome c. Disruption of Bcl2 allows for cytochrome c to leave the mitochondria and activate apoptosis.
Blc2 is overexpressed in follicular lymphoma. t(14;18) swaps IgH locus with Bcl2, leading to increased expression of Bcl2. Mitochondrial membrane is further stabilized, prohibiting apoptosis. B cells that would normally undergo apoptosis during somatic hypermutation in the lymph node germinal center accumulate, leading ot lymphoma.

Other Important Features of Tumor Development

Telomerase is necessary for cell immortality. Normally, telomeres shorten with serial cell divisions, eventually resulting in cellular senescence. Cancers often have upregulated telomerase, which preserves telomeres.

Angiogenesis is necessary for tumor survival and growth. FGF and VEGF are commonly produced by tumor cells.

Avoiding immune surveillance is necessary for tumor survival. Mutations often result in production of abnormal proteins, which are expressed on MHCⅠ. CD8+ T cells detect and destroy such mutated cells. Tumor cells can evade immune surveillance by downregulating expression of MCHⅠ. Immunodeficiency (both primary and secondary) increases risk for cancer.

Tumor Progression

Tumor Invasion and Spread

Accumulation of mutations eventually results in tumor invasion and spread. Epithelial tumor are normally attached to one another by cellular adhesion molecules (e.g., E‑cadherin). Downregulation of E‑cadherin leads to dissociation of attached cells. Cells attach to laminin and destroy basement membrane (collagen Ⅳ) via collagenase. Cells attached to fibronectin in the ECM and spread locally. Entrance into blood or lymph vessels allows for metastasis.

Routes of Metastasis

Lymphatic spread is characterized of carcinoma.

Hematogenous spread is characteristics of sarcomas and some carcinomas (renal cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, follicular carcinoma of thyroid, choriocarcinoma).

Seeding of body cavities is characteristic of ovarian carcinoma, which often involves the peritoneum ('omental caking').

Clinical Characteristics

Clinical Features

Benign tumors tend to be slow growing, well circumscribed, distinct, and mobile. Malignant tumors are usually rapid growing, poorly circumscribed, infiltrative, and fixed to surrounding tissues and local structures. Biopsy or excision is generally require to classify a tumor definitively. Some benign tumor can grow in a malignant-like fashion, and vice versa.

Histological Features

Serum Tumor Markers

Grading of Cancer

The grading of a tumor is a pathology determination of tumor based on the degree of differentiation of the tumor. Generally, low grade tumors are well-differentiated, and high-grade tumors are poorly differentiation. Higher grade tumors are frequently associated with worse prognosis.

Staging of Cancer

The staging of a tumor is a clinical and pathology determination based on: (1) the gross and microscopic features of the tumors (e.g., size, grade) [T], (2) presence or absence of regional lymph nodes involvement [N], (3) presence or absence of distant metastases [M].

  • T - dependent on the tissue/organ afflicted
    • For example, in the lungs, less than 3 cm is T1. Three to seven centimeters is T2. Larger than 7 or multiple nodules less than 7 cm is T3.
  • N0 - no nodes. N1 - nearby nodes. N2 - further spread but still regional. N3 - spread far away
  • M0 - no mets, M1 - mets

Collectively, this is called TNM staging, which is a pathology determination. The clinical staging of tumors (stages Ⅰ through Ⅳ) is roughly correlates with TNM staging, where Stage Ⅰ is localized tumor and Stage Ⅳ is metastatic cancer.
