Bacterial Staining

Gram stain

Gram staining is performed as follow:

  1. Addition of crystal violet stain
  2. Addition of iodine - traps crystal violet in Gram ⊕︀ organisms
  3. Wash by alcohol - removes iodine; doesn't work in Gram ⊕︀
  4. Safranin counterstain - visualize Gram ⊝

Acid-fast stain

  • Distinguishes mycobacteria, all of which are acid-fast ⊕︀, appearing red on the stain
  • Uses carbol-fuschin dye to stain mycolic acid, present in the cell walls of mycobacteria

Classification by Gram staining

Gram ⊕︀ organisms

See Gram ⊕︀ bacteria for more information.

A Gram ⊕︀ bacterium has a single layer cell membrane and a thick, multilayered, highly cross-linked peptidoglycan cell wall, which gives them their positive staining. These bacteria also possess teichoic acids which can be linked to the cell membrane (as lipoteichoic acids) or the peptidoglycans in the cell wall. The lipoteichoic acid is unique to Gram ⊕︀ bacteria and plays a role in adherence and Gram-positive shock.

Examples of such organisms include: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus agalactiae, Streptococcus pyogenes, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Listeria monocytogenes, Clostridium spp., and Clostridiodes difficile. These organisms are well-known to infect the respiratory system, ENT, dental caries, urinary tract, large intestine, and neonatal meninges.

Gram ⊝ organisms

See Gram ⊝ bacteria for more information.

Gram ⊝ bacteria have double cell membrane with a thin peptidoglycan cell wall in between. Within their outer cell membrane is lipopolysaccharide (LPS) which consists of O-antigen, core polysaccharide, and lipid A—an endotoxin (b/c it is not actively released by the bacteria). This lipid A is responsible for many of the symptoms of a Gram ⊝ infection including fever, hypotension, histamine release, and DICa severe condition where blood clots form uncontrollably, depleting clotting factors and causing dangerous bleeding. Another key clinical feature of Gram ⊝ bacteria is the presence of β‑lactamases, conferring resistance to β‑lactam antibiotics (e.g, PCNs, cephalosporins, etc.).

Examples of such organisms include: Escherichia coli, Neisseria spp., Haemophilus influenzae, Bordetella pertussis, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Salmonella spp., Shigella, Klebsiella, Campylobacter jejuni, Helicobacter pylori, and Vibrio cholerae. The bacteria typically infect the GI tract, the respiratory tract, and the urinary tract.

Non-Gram organisms

See Non-Gram bacteria for more information.

Some bacteria don't appear under Gram stain at all (neither violet for ⊕︀ nor red for ⊝). These include: Legionella pneumophilia, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Rickettsia spp., Treponema pallidum, Gardenerella vaginalis, and Chlamydia trachomatis.
