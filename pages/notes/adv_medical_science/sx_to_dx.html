Common Symptoms

Cough

Fever and cough make up a clinical syndrome that generally is associated with infections of the upper respiratory tract (bronchitis) and/or the lower respiratory tract (pneumonia, PNA). The differential diagnosis for acute infections of the respiratory tract is extremely broad and ranges from extremely common, mild infections to rare, life‑threatening infections (e.g., Pneumocystis, Aspergillus).

Pneumonia (PNA)

In general, the dx of PNA is based on symptoms (fever, cough, sputum production, dyspnea, chest pain) and signs (fever, tachypnea, hypoxemia, rales, egophony) in combination with new pulmonary infiltrate on chest imaging (typically CXR or sometimes chest CT).

Sore throat

The diagnosis of acute tonsillitis or acute pharyngitis is primarily clinical. The first step is to look for red‑flag features, which can indicate a life‑threatening etiology. If such feature(s) are present, consult ENT and proceed with treatment. Otherwise, continue to assess for acute bacterial or viral infection and perform the appropriate diagnostic test:

  • Rapid strep test, if suspect bacterial tonsillopharyngitis
  • EBV serology or heterophile antibody test, if suspect infectious mononucleosis
  • COVID‑19 test, if suspect COVID‑19
Red‑flag features Life‑threatening causes
  • Trismus Limited ability to open jaw d/t to tonic spasm of the jaw muscles. Most commonly associated with tetanus. Can also arise 2° to peritonsillar abscess, infection in jaw muscles, and trigeminal nerve pathology.
  • Drooling
  • Asymmetric tonsils
  • Displaced uvula
  • Unilateral facial swelling
  • Muffled or "hot potato" voice
  • Clinical features of sepsis (fever, tachycardia, signs of organ dysfunction, hypotension (if in shock))
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Ludwig angina
  • Epiglottitis
  • Retropharyngeal abscess
  • Bacterial tracheitis
  • Peritonsillar abscess
  • Vincent angina

Nasal Congestion

Nasal congestion is a common chief concern that is best assessed by detailed history and physical examination. When interviewing the patient, consider multiple etiologies including allergic/inflammatory (allergic rhinitis, chronic sinusitis), infection (viral, bacterial), anatomical (deviated septum, turbinate hypertrophy), traumatic, and drug‑induced (cocaine, intranasal medications). Duration and timing of symptoms are important factors to consider because causes may vary based on how long the patient has experienced symptoms, precipitating factor, and time of year.

Nasal congestion occurs due to inflammation of nasal epithelia and production of mucus, making it difficult to breath through the nose. Nasal congestion may causes additional symptoms such as rhinorrhea (runny nose), postnasal drip, sneezing, cough, and headache. The causes of nasal congestion include:

  • Allergic rhinitis
  • Nonallergic rhinitis
    • Viral rhinitis (rhinovirus (common cold), flu, COVID‑19)
    • Cold temperatures
    • Sinusitis
    • Nasal polyps
    • Nasal foreign body
    • Enlarged adenoids
    • Environmental irritants
    • Pregnancy rhinitis
    • Medications
      • Birth control, BP meds, antidepressants, ED meds
      • Opioid withdrawal
    • CSF leak

Headache

Headaches are one of the most common causes for patients to seek medical attention and may require evaluation and management in the emergency room and outpatient settings. In approaching a patient with a headache, obtaining A detailed history identifying key patterns in clinical features will help with categorization of headaches into primary or secondary causes and will guide you towards a diagnosis and appropriate evaluation and treatment. Primary headaches are common presentations in the outpatient setting. Secondary headaches are caused by other ideologies, including space occupying lesions (e.g., tumors, hematomas, hydrocephalus), severe prolonged hypertension, traumatic injury, and infection (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis). Space occupying lesions will also typically result in increased intracranial pressure, which can present with additional findings such as papilledema, nausea and vomiting, impaired consciousness, and Cushing reflex with irregular breathing, increased blood pressure, and bradycardia. Several causes of secondary headaches occur acutely, and these can be life threatening emergencies.

Headache with Fever

Headache and fever in combination are concerning for infections involving the CNS and should prompt immediate evaluation, diagnosis, and treatment. A variety of pathogens can cause infeciton of the brain, the meninges, or the spinal cord. The presentations of the patients infected with different pathogens may be quite similar. Brain abcess are space‑occupying infections that can also present with fever and headache and likewise can be d/t to a host of pathogens. A thorough evaluation of risk factors for given pathogens, as well as CNS imaging and CSF sampling, is paramount to making an accurate diagnosis.
